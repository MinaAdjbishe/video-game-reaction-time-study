# The Impact of Video Game Experience on Response Time  
*A Go/No-Go Task Examining Cognitive Performance*

## Overview  
This project investigates whether different levels of video game experience influence reaction time during a Go/No-Go task. Participants were grouped as **non‑gamers**, **casual gamers**, and **experienced gamers**, and their reaction times were compared using a one‑way ANOVA. Although experienced gamers responded slightly faster on average, the differences were **not statistically significant**.

The study contributes to ongoing research on how gaming relates to attention, processing speed, and inhibitory control.

## Research Questions  
- Do gamers respond faster than non‑gamers?  
- Does gaming experience level influence reaction time?  
- Are descriptive differences supported by statistical evidence?

## Method  
- **Design:** Between‑groups quasi‑experimental  
- **Independent Variable:** Gaming experience (non‑gamer, casual gamer, experienced gamer)  
- **Dependent Variable:** Mean reaction time (ms)  
- **Participants:** 55 adults  
- **Task:** 72‑trial Go/No-Go task delivered via Gorilla Experiment Builder  
- **Analysis:** One‑way ANOVA  

## Results  
- **Experienced gamers:** 354 ms  
- **Non‑gamers:** 375 ms  
- **Casual gamers:** 390 ms  
- **ANOVA:** F(2, 52) = 2.20, p = .12 → *not significant*  
- Overlapping variability across groups suggests no reliable performance differences.

## Interpretation  
The findings suggest that **general gaming experience alone may not reliably enhance reaction time**. Previous research often focuses on *action* gamers, who may show stronger cognitive effects. This study highlights the importance of considering:

- game genre  
- task sensitivity  
- individual differences  
- accuracy of self‑reported gaming habits  

Future research should incorporate more detailed gaming measures and larger samples.
